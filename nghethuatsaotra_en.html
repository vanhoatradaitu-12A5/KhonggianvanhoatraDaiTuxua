The Art of Tea Roasting

The secret to preserving the young green rice aroma & the sweet aftertaste of the Dai Tu land

Back to Essence of Viet Tea

The Soul of Dai Tu Tea

If soil and climate are gifts from nature, then the tea roasting technique is the soul breathed in by humans. In Dai Tu, roasting tea is not simply drying tea leaves, but an art of controlling fire and sensing with every sense.

The artisan must use bare hands to feel the temperature, use the sense of smell to "listen" to the scent changing every second, and use generations of experience to decide the golden moment.

The "Four Great Stages" Process

1 Fixing (Kill Green)

This is the most important stage to keep the green color. Fresh tea leaves are put into a scorching cast-iron pan or rotary drum (250-300°C) for a short time to stop enzymatic oxidation, making the leaves soft, pliable, and retaining their chlorophyll green color.

2 Rolling

After fixing, the tea is rolled. The artisan skillfully rolls so that the tea leaves curl tightly into the characteristic hook shape without crushing the leaves. This process breaks the leaf cells so that when brewed, the tea essence easily dissolves into the water.

3 Drying

After rolling, the tea is put back into the pan to dry. At this point, the fire must be controlled extremely carefully, gradually reducing the temperature. The roaster must stir evenly and continuously so the tea dries evenly and doesn't burn, ensuring the tea water remains clear and green later.

4 Scent Enhancement

The final and most delicate stage. When the tea is dry, the artisan slightly increases the heat to awaken the scent. This is when the passionate young green rice aroma is released. Just a second of overheating, the scent will vanish, and the tea will turn red.
