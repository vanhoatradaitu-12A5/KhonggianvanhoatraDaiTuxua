The essence of Vietnamese Tea

The origins of Tea culture

"More than just a drink, it is the flow of history."

The story dates back to ancient times. Legend has it that a Royal Consort of King Hùng Duệ Vương—the 17th Hùng King and the final ruler of the Văn Lang Kingdom (circa 334 – 258 BC)—was the mother-in-law of two legendary figures in Vietnamese history: Chử Đồng Tử (husband of Princess Tiên Dung) and Sơn Tinh (husband of Princess Ngọc Hoa). It is said that this Royal Consort returned to Văn Luông Village (known today as Văn Phú, Phú Thọ Province) to teach the locals how to cultivate tea and cotton as a livelihood. Since then, people from all over gathered to settle and establish villages, eventually forming Bãi Chè (The Tea Field Hamlet) and Xóm Bông (The Cotton Hamlet) to honor her legacy and contributions.

Throughout our nation's history of building and defense, tea culture has stood as a living witness to generations of heroes. Despite the ebb and flow of history and political upheavals, this cultural beauty has not only survived but thrived, cultivating a unique charm of its own. It is these distinct qualities that have added vibrant and proud strokes to the tapestry of Vietnamese culture. In the past, there were eras when tea was revered as a luxury reserved solely for royalty, the nobility, and the elite. Naturally, this was accompanied by elaborate rituals of brewing and tasting. The etiquette of serving tea was equally demanding, requiring absolute reverence and respect toward elders and superiors. Over time, however, Vietnamese tea culture evolved. It spread to every corner of life, transcending boundaries of wealth and social status. Whether in a noble household or a commoner's home, at a humble roadside stall or a fine dining establishment, a cup of tea has become a familiar and essential part of daily life.

Vietnamese tea culture is like a gentle stream, flowing quietly yet persistently through the currents of history, forever residing in the hearts of the descendants of the Lạc Hồng bloodline. It stands as a living witness, a bridge connecting the past and the present, and a steadfast spiritual anchor for the Vietnamese people.

From favorable conditions to Vietnam's Tea capital

It is no coincidence that Thai Nguyen is hailed as the country's 'Tea Capital.' This title reflects the perfect convergence of nature's gifts, long-standing farming traditions, and the dexterous hands of tea artisans.

Ideal soil- Climate

Compared to other crops, the tea plant is not overly demanding regarding soil conditions. However, to ensure optimal growth and high, stable yields, the soil must meet specific criteria: it must be fertile, rich in humus, and possess adequate depth, appropriate pH, and good drainage. The optimal pH range for tea cultivation is 4.5 – 6.0. Additionally, the soil depth must be at least 80 cm, and the groundwater table should be below 1 meter to allow the root system to develop properly.

The soil in the Thai Nguyen midlands is characterized by red-yellow ferralitic properties: it is porous, rich in humus, and well-drained—making it highly suitable for the tea plant's root system. The region's humid subtropical climate, defined by early morning mists, mild temperatures, and an average annual rainfall of 1,500–2,000 mm, creates the perfect environment for the tea to develop its exquisite flavor.

A long-standing tradition of cultivation

With diligent and hardworking hands, the people of Dai Tu have cultivated countless lush green tea hills, establishing this land as one of the largest and most historic tea-growing regions in Thai Nguyen. This tradition has been passed down from generation to generation, creating a richness and diversity in every cup through precious tea varieties such as native Tea, hybrid Tea, Long Van tea... all of which hold immense value.

The craft of traditional Tea roasting

The people of Thai Nguyen possess a unique hand-roasting technique: using cast-iron pans, manually controlling the fire, sensing the tea’s 'doneness' with bare hands, and preserving the heat in cotton sacks. These steps, requiring the utmost subtlety and refinement, create the signature aroma of young green rice —an unmistakable scent found nowhere else but in Thai Nguyen tea.

Cultivation and care process

1. Cultivation

Soil preparation, the selection of tea cultivars (Native tea, F1 hybrid...), and planting techniques ensuring optimal density serve as the foundation for the sustainable growth of the tea plants.

2. Care process

Irrigation: Tea plants require an adequate amount of water for growth, especially during the sapling stage (or 'young plant stage'). During the dry season, consistent irrigation is necessary, typically 2–3 times per week, to maintain soil moisture.

Fertilization: We prioritize the use of organic fertilizer and organic microbial fertilizer to enhance both the yield and the quality of the tea plants. Fertilization is carried out in 3–4 applications annually, primarily during the spring and the start of the rainy season.

Pest and Disease Management: It is essential to monitor and care for the tea plants regularly, while continuously honing knowledge on tea plant pest and disease management, to ensure optimal development and high yield.

Processing Stages

"To produce tea products of exquisite quality, artisans must possess seasoned experience to finely tune every stage of the crafting process, ensuring it perfectly suits each specific tea variety."

Step 1: Withering

After harvesting, the tea leaves undergo a natural withering process. This step aims to evaporate surface dew and dissipate the heat and humidity accumulated during transport from the tea hills. The leaves are typically spread evenly on large traditional bamboo trays (nong, nia) or on a clean, cool, and well-ventilated surface. The layer thickness is maintained at 15–20 cm. The duration of the withering process varies depending on weather conditions.

Step 2: Fixation (Kill-green)

Also locally known as 'op che, this step involves placing the leaves into a hot rotating drum after the withering phase. The purpose of fixation is to halt oxidation (cook the leaves), reducing bitterness and eliminating the raw, grassy taste (vị ngái). Upon completion, the initial pungent odor is replaced by the tea's signature fragrance. The leaves transform from bright green to a darker green and become soft and pliable. A key indicator of perfection is when the leaves and buds, gently twisted by hand, do not exude any surface moisture.

Step 3: Rolling

This is the crucial step that determines the aesthetic and structural integrity of the finished tea leaves. The duration of the rolling process is adjusted to suit the specific final product, such as Móc Câu (hook-shaped), Tôm Nõn (shrimp bud), or Chè Đinh (nail tea). In the past, artisans performed the rolling entirely by hand. Today, with advancements in technology, the process begins with a manual pre-roll to eliminate broken fragments. The leaves are then transferred to the rolling machine (cối vò) and processed until they meet the total time requirement.

Step 4: Loosening

Once the rolling time is complete, the artisan proceeds to loosen the tea leaves. This process of unclumping is essential for heat dissipation and preventing the leaves from forming clumps, thereby ensuring uniformity in quality for the subsequent processing stages.

Step 5: Drying

This is a critical stage that greatly impacts the final quality of the tea product. To achieve peak tea quality, the artisans perform the final firing with immense care, executing the process twice using wood-fired pans.

Step 6: Aroma enhancement & finishing roast

Once the tea has rested and stabilized in quality, it undergoes the fragrance enhancement and finishing roast. This crucial heating process helps break down the tannin and ester bonds within the leaves. The result is a tea with a gentle astringency rather than harsh bitterness, a lingering sweet aftertaste, and the signature fresh aroma of Young Green Rice (Hương Cốm) upon brewing.

Step 7 : Packaging and Preservation

After the final roasting, the tea is allowed to cool before being sorted and packaged according to market specifications. For long-term storage or delayed consumption, the tea is vacuum-sealed to preserve its freshness.
